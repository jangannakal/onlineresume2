An Advanced TCM Practitioner Reading List

My TCM library is a sort of refuge for me. A place I retreat to when I can, although not as often as I would like. There remains a desire to commit myself completely to its study, outside the distractions and demands of everyday life. And yet, it is these demands and distractions which, out of necessity for solution, lead me back to the books:

  • My baby son has indigestion with constipation.
  • My grandmother-in-law falls ill with vertigo and vomiting.
  • A friend of a friend has a wound that will not heal.
  • What is the thermal nature of douchi?

The 10,000 things require review of the theoretical frameworks which regulate them so they might be solved.

So, here is a list of indispensible sources which better serve the practitioner who has already done the work building strong TCM theoretical foundations. It is not complete by any means but serves as a starting point to drill deeper as wanted or needed.

The Seven Emotions: Psychology and Health in Ancient China

This could be given to any lay person as a guidebook to contextualising their emotional lives through the lens of organ and meridian theory. Welcome to the intellectual prowess of the Jesuit priest Larre and Mrs. Rochat de la Vallee.

The Eight Extraordinary Meridians

Demystify the hype around the 奇 經 八 脈 and learn to better serve your patients by knowing and using them. More amazing work from Rochat Larre.

Handbook of TCM Pediatrics

As a new father, caring for my infant son in his first year of life brought on unanticipated challenges I was able to successfully navigate with the guidance of Bob Flaws. Used in tandem with Chinese sources, it points the way into TCM pediatrics.

Master Tong's Acupuncture: An Ancient Alternative Style in Modern Clinical Practice

A Singaporean TCM doctor in New Zealand introduced me to this book. Sceptical at first but had success with pain management in patients suffering from sciatica and those dying from cancer.

Anatomy Trains

Required reading to deepen your understanding of meridian theory. Myer's wet specimen dissections and anatomical segmentations are a work of the highest pedigree.

The Dr. Langevin TCM-NIH studies

Dr. Helene Langevin (M.D) is director at the NIH's National Center for Complimentary and
Integrative Health. Never let anyone say there is no peer-reviewed research on TCM in the west. Dr. Langevin broke the ice for all of us. Her publication on the reliable quantifying of yin and yang is mandatory reading.

Shang Han Lun: On Cold Damage, Translation & Commentaries

One of the oldest complete clinical textbooks in the world, written sometime before 220 AD. Your guidebook to understanding how elemental factors penetrate the body and cause disease.

The theoretical foundations of Chinese medicine: systems of correspondence

As if TCM study is not challenging enough, I introduce Manfred Porkert to the mix. His prose is robust and unmerciful but the revealed insights make the literary beatdown well worth it.
